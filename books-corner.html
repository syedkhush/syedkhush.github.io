Book Corner

The books I loved and why they stayed with me

I don't read fast. I read slowly, with a pen nearby. These are the books that changed something in the way I see the world — not because they were popular, but because they were true to something I already felt but couldn't yet name.

01
Philosophy · Stoicism · Self-mastery
Meditations
Marcus Aurelius — translated by Gregory Hays
★★★★★
"You have power over your mind, not outside events. Realise this, and you will find strength."

This book was never meant to be published. Marcus Aurelius — Roman Emperor, the most powerful man alive at the time — wrote it to himself. Not to impress anyone. Not to build a legacy. Just to remind himself, daily, how to be a better human being. That alone made me trust it completely.

I read it at a point in my life when I was fighting with anger constantly. Small things would set me off. The traffic. Someone's tone. Plans that didn't work out. I was reacting to everything and exhausting myself doing it. Then I picked up this book and something shifted.

Marcus kept writing one thing over and over in different ways: you cannot control what happens outside you, only how you respond to it. He was emperor of Rome and he still had to remind himself of this every single day. That hit me hard. If the most powerful man in the world had to train his mind daily to stay calm — what was my excuse?

After reading Meditations, I started catching myself before reacting. Someone says something sharp — I pause. A plan collapses — I pause. Not because I became emotionless, but because I started asking: is this worth my energy? Is this in my control? Most of the time, the answer was no. And that answer changed everything.

The chaos didn't disappear from my life. But I stopped feeding it with my anger. That's the whole book, really. Written two thousand years ago by a man who is long dead — and it's still the most practical thing I've ever read.

What I took from it Anger is a tax you pay on things outside your control. The moment I understood that, I stopped paying it as often. Read this slowly — one page at a time, like he wrote it.
